Welko

About

Welko is a mobile app that connects users to affordable community resources, livestream wellness classes and a customizable health journal.

Role

Sole UX Designer & Researcher

Duration

5 months

Tools

Figma, Miro, Optimal Sort, UsabilityHub, Google Forms, Keynote, Numbers, Zoom, Post-Its, Paper & Pen

What's the problem?

Health-conscious adults need a way to take ownership over their own physical, emotional and mental health because information in today’s healthcare age can be inaccessible, unreliable and unaffordable.

Where's the gap?

According to the World Health Organization, COVID-19 “has disrupted or halted critical mental health services in 93% of countries worldwide while the demand for mental health is increasing.” Today’s wellness apps focus solely on health portals or mindfulness and meditation. There is an opportunity to combine the two into one app.

What's the solution?

Welko bridges the gap between personal wellbeing and healthcare by connecting users to affordable and accessible wellness resources within their community. Our app will help individuals to improve their emotional, mental and physical wellbeing by providing 24-hour livestream classes and a customizable health journal.

Design Process

Discovery

I conducted 7 interviews and surveyed 36 heath-conscious individuals and wellness professionals to understand what motivates and frustrates people when it comes to personal wellbeing. I asked interviewees about their perspective shifts regarding personal health during the COVID-19 pandemic to uncover awareness of resources in their communities, including mental health clinics and testing sites. 41.7 % of those surveyed said connecting with others in their community played a great deal into their idea of personal health and wellbeing. 47.2% said it played somewhat of a role with 11.1% saying wellbeing is a personal issue. I separated insights from interviews and the survey into 3 categories: user needs, frustrations and motivators.

Personas

Welko has 3 main personas. Each wants to find or build community in a different way — through accessing local wellness resources, attending or leading a class.

Logan

Self-motivated, health enthusiast, 35 years old, Program Manager

Needs & Goals

  • Connect social circle remotely each week

Frustrations

  • Heightened anxiety during pandemic
  • Confusing medical portals and wellness apps

Motivators

  • Making routines
  • Returning to in-person fitness classes

Blake

Unsure, busy & health-aware, 26 years old, Graduate student

Needs & Goals

  • Find affordable mental health resources
  • Keep track of personal health records

Frustrations

  • Depression
  • Expensive and confusing healthcare system

Motivators

  • Spending alone time outside, away from tech

Charlie

Encouraging, wellness instructor, 40 years old, Meditation teacher

Needs & Goals

  • Keep students engaged virtually
  • Build community of like-minded meditators and teachers

Frustrations

  • Zoom fatigue
  • Transitioning to remote teaching
  • Inconsistent income

Motivators

  • Sharing passion for wellness with others
  • Growing online and in-person meditation communities

Card Sort

I conducted an open card sort with 10 health-conscious individuals and 27 cards to understand how users would group the 4 main features of Welko. Here’s what I discovered:

  1. Most participants grouped “Connect to health care provider” with Community terms. I will need to ensure a separation between Health and Community content.
  2. I need to revise the sitemap and combine all Class* content into one section as a majority of participants didn’t distinguish between live and archive class cards.
  3. Most sorters grouped settings, account, profile and onboarding features together into one category.
  4. Health* features were the least consistently grouped amongst the 4 features. It will be important to consider distinctions users make between Health and Community content.

Prototypes

I created low, mid and high-fidelity prototypes for 4 flows: onboarding, adding a community resource to favorites, creating a journal entry and adding a live class to schedule. I used a combination of feedback from user interviews, usability sessions and design critiques to iterate each version.

Testing

I interviewed 6 adult participants during remote and in-person moderated usability sessions. Each participant represents one of our 2 health-conscious personas, Blake or Logan. I used affinity maps to organize observations, errors and quotes—negative and positive —from usability tests.

Takeaways

  1. User Testing

    Test with people who reflect the persona(s). It will be easier to defend design decisions with stakeholders if testers are motivated and frustrated by the same things that personas are.

  2. Prototyping

    Include micro-interactions in prototypes, like tapping an input field to make a keyboard appear or adding text to input fields. This will help interactions feel realistic and lead to more valuable user feedback.

  3. Iterative Design

    Spend adequate time in the discovery and ideation phases to set a strong foundation for all design iterations. Back up decisions with a mix of qualitative and quantitative data, such as preference test results and direct quotes from usability sessions.
